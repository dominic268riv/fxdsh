📖 Glossary of Terms
Market rates
Spot rate
The current exchange rate for immediate delivery (technically T+2 business days). The baseline from which forward rates are derived.
e.g. EUR/USD spot of 1.1532 means €1 buys $1.1532 today.
Pip
The smallest standard unit of price movement in FX. For most pairs it's the 4th decimal place (0.0001). For JPY pairs it's the 2nd decimal place (0.01).
e.g. EUR/USD moving from 1.1532 to 1.1533 is a move of 1 pip.
Forward points (fwd points)
The difference between the forward rate and the spot rate, expressed in pips. Positive points mean the forward trades at a premium to spot; negative means a discount. Driven entirely by the interest rate differential between the two currencies.
e.g. EUR/USD spot 1.1532, fair forward 1.1574 → forward points = +42 pips.
Forward premium / discount
The forward points expressed as a percentage of the spot rate. A premium means the base currency (left side of the pair) is worth more in the future; a discount means it's worth less.
e.g. +42 pips on EUR/USD spot 1.1532 = +0.36% premium.
Bid / Ask spread
The difference between the price a market maker will buy (bid) and sell (ask) a currency. The spread is how the interbank market makes money. Your desk then adds its own margin on top.
Pricing & the forward
Fair forward (mid)
The theoretically correct forward rate calculated using Covered Interest Parity (CIP). It is the rate at which no risk-free arbitrage profit is possible between investing in one currency versus the other. Calculated as: Spot × e^((r_quote − r_base) × T).
e.g. EUR/USD spot 1.1532, SOFR 3.60%, €STR 2.15%, 91 days → fair forward 1.1574.
Covered Interest Parity (CIP)
The no-arbitrage condition that links spot rates, forward rates, and interest rates. It states that the forward rate must reflect the interest rate differential between two currencies — otherwise traders could lock in a risk-free profit.
Strike rate
The agreed exchange rate locked into the forward contract. For a new trade, this is typically the client all-in rate (fair forward ± sales margin). For an existing trade, it's the rate booked at inception — used to calculate the current MtM.
e.g. Acme books a EUR/USD forward at a strike of 1.1574 — that's the rate they'll transact at on the value date regardless of where spot is.
Tenor
The length of time until the forward contract matures and settlement occurs. Standard tenors are 1W, 1M, 3M, 6M, 1Y etc. Longer tenors carry more interest rate risk and typically wider bid-ask spreads.
Value date (settlement date)
The date on which the actual exchange of currencies takes place. For spot it's T+2; for forwards it's the agreed future date.
Notional
The face value of the contract — the amount of the buy currency being exchanged. Revenue and MtM are both calculated as a function of notional.
Counter value
The equivalent amount in the sell currency at the fair forward mid rate. This is what the client pays or receives on the sell side at maturity.
e.g. €5M notional at a forward of 1.1574 → counter value of $5,787,000.
Risk-free rate (RFR)
The overnight interest rate for a given currency, published daily by the relevant central bank or administrator. Used in CIP to calculate the fair forward. Replaced LIBOR after 2021. Key RFRs: SOFR (USD), SONIA (GBP), €STR (EUR), TONA (JPY), SARON (CHF), CORRA (CAD).
Mark-to-market & risk
Mark-to-market (MtM)
The current value of an existing forward contract if it were unwound today. Calculated as the difference between the current fair forward and the original strike rate, multiplied by notional, discounted back to today. Positive MtM means the trade is in the client's favour; negative means it's offside.
e.g. Client struck at 1.1680, fair forward today is 1.1574 → client is offside by 106 pips, MtM is negative.
Discount factor
The factor used to bring a future cash flow back to its present value. Calculated as e^(−r × T). The MtM is discounted because the profit or loss is only realised at the value date, not today.
Revenue & commercial
Sales margin
The number of pips your desk adds to (or subtracts from) the fair forward mid rate to arrive at the client rate. This is your primary source of revenue on FX forwards.
e.g. Fair forward 1.1574, 12 pip margin → client rate 1.1586 (client receives fewer USD per EUR).
Client all-in rate
The final rate shown to the client, inclusive of your sales margin. This is the rate that appears on the trade confirmation.
Gross revenue
The total cash revenue your desk earns on the trade, in sell currency terms. Calculated as sales margin (in rate terms) × notional.
e.g. 12 pips × €5,000,000 = $6,000 gross revenue.
Basis points (bps) on notional value
Your margin expressed as a fraction of the total notional value, in hundredths of a percent. Useful for comparing revenue across trades of different sizes and currency pairs on a like-for-like basis. 1 bp = 0.01%.
e.g. $6,000 revenue on $5.79M counter value = 1.04 bps.
Revenue per 1M notional
The gross revenue normalised to a €1M (or equivalent) notional. Makes it easy to quickly compare the profitability of different trade types regardless of deal size.
Deal types
Outright forward
A single forward contract to buy or sell a fixed amount of currency at a fixed rate on a fixed future date. The most common corporate FX hedging instrument.
Window forward
A forward that can be drawn down (settled) at any point within a date range rather than on a single fixed date. Useful for clients with uncertain cash flow timing. Priced at the worst-case (most expensive) date in the window.
Non-deliverable forward (NDF)
A forward contract for currencies that are restricted or illiquid where physical delivery is not possible (e.g. CNH, INR, BRL). Instead of exchanging principal, only the difference between the contracted rate and the fixing rate is settled in USD.
FX swap
A simultaneous spot purchase and forward sale (or vice versa) of a currency. Used to roll an existing forward to a new date, or to borrow one currency against another. The price is quoted purely in forward points.
